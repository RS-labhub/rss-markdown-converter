The cybersecurity landscape for connected devices is fundamentally broken.

Most IoT devices ship with security as an afterthought. Default passwords, unencrypted communications, and no update mechanisms create massive attack surfaces that scale with device deployment.

The cost of fixing security issues post-deployment is 100x higher than building security in from the start. Yet most teams still treat security as a compliance checkbox rather than a core engineering requirement.

This needs to change. The upcoming EU Cyber Resilience Act is forcing this conversation, but regulation shouldn't be our primary motivation.

Every connected device is a potential entry point into critical infrastructure. We have a responsibility to build secure systems from the ground up.

The tools and knowledge exist. The question is whether we'll use them before regulation forces our hand.

𝗔 𝗿𝗲𝗮𝗹 𝗰𝗼𝗻𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘀𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗮𝗯𝗼𝘂𝘁 𝗔𝗜 𝗶𝗻 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗱𝘂𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻:

"Our AI feature works great in demos but fails randomly in production. The model gives different outputs for the same inputs, and we can't figure out why."

This is the most common challenge I hear from enterprise teams implementing AI. The gap between prototype and production is massive, and most teams are unprepared for it.

Some hard-learned lessons from teams that have crossed this gap:
✦ Version control everything - prompts, examples, model configs
✦ Build comprehensive evaluation frameworks from day one
✦ Implement gradual rollouts with clear rollback procedures
✦ Monitor AI outputs as rigorously as any other system metric

AI isn't just another API you integrate. It's a fundamentally different type of system that requires new engineering disciplines.
